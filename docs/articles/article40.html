The Italian steel industry is facing a major challenge with the ongoing crisis at the former Ilva steel mill in Taranto. The association Aigi, which represents about 80% of the companies that work with Ilva, has called for government intervention to ensure the payment of outstanding debts and prevent the closure of the plant. On Wednesday, a corteo consisting of workers and vehicles departed from the plant's C loop and marched towards the city, causing temporary roadblocks and traffic slowdowns on the state highways 106 Ionica and Appia. The protest was sparked despite the Minister of Industry and Made in Italy Adolfo Urso having convened a videoconference with the associations representing Ilva's suppliers and industry partners on Tuesday to discuss the situation and possible solutions. Aigi is demanding the payment of all invoices due by December 31, 2023, and is calling for a "credible industrial plan" that guarantees continuity in production. The protest was held despite the Italian government's intention to appoint a temporary commissioner to resolve the Ilva crisis and find new partners for the plant's operation. The Italian Senate will begin examining the decree on Ilva, which was published in Gazzetta Ufficiale on Wednesday. The decree strengthens existing measures to protect the continuity of production and employment at struggling companies, including Ilva, and provides for cash transfers during any potential amministrazione straordinaria. The European Union is also weighing in on the Ilva crisis, with the Commission's spokesperson stating that Italy must decide whether a specific measure involves state aid and therefore must be notified to the Commission for preventive evaluation of any concession of aid. In response to this challenge, the Italian steel industry is calling for greater support from the government and the international community to ensure the survival of Ilva and maintain the country's strategic position in the global steel market.
